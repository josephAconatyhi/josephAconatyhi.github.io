Maryland Drivers: Are We Really THAT Bad?

by Joseph Conaty

Police and Emergency Response to an accident in Laurel, Maryland. Photo by Joseph Conaty.

Have you ever been cut off on the freeway and thought to yourself, "What a Maryland Driver..."? Well, if you have them you're not alone, because practically everyone I know from family members and friends to complete strangers believe that Maryland drivers are terrible. What I want to know is can we find statistical evidence to show that Maryland drivers really ARE that bad? Let's find out.

Using data from 2019 to 2023, Maryland drivers get into around 107,886 car crashes a year. Out of these roughly 108,000 accidents, 535 were fatalities while 28,609 of them led to injuries. Here is a visual breakdown of the 5 year data averages:

Now, where does Maryland stack up compared to the rest of the country? Well, according to a LendingTree survey from 2024, Maryland is rated 8th worst in the nation for accidents per 1000 drivers. We have 24.3 accidents per 1,000 drivers which is not as high as Massachusetts with 44.4 accidents but is far off the median of 14.8 accidents.

So according to their stats Maryland is home to the 8th worst drivers in America. Here's a visual of Maryland's accident rate comapred to the two highest and lowest states for reference:

Now let's compare to our neighbors on the East coast - Virginia and Delaware. Virginia is 22nd in the nation at 26.7 accidents and Delaware is 24th at 26.2. And right in out backyard, Washington D.C. stands at 7th worst in the nation with 37.4 accidents per 1,000 drivers.

Well that at face value proves that Maryland drivers are in fact bad drivers, but there are questions to sift through still. First is are we top 10 in all incidents? Second, why are Marylanders this high in accidents per 1,000 drivers? Is it something to do with the bar being low to pass the MVA tests? And what can we do to as a state get better at driving?



So how can we change the "Maryland Driver" narrative?

Most articles I see when I ask these questions have to do with what the individual driver can do better, "defensive driving" and such. But I think improvement has to start in the tests. In personal experience, there wasn't enough emphasis on technique and actual experience on highways or on the road in programs that you have to take after getting provisional. So I think statewide driving programs need to get drivers more experience on high intensity situations like the highway, especially since that's where so much of Maryland transportation takes place.

This is data taken from a Deep Creek Times article that measured nation wide driving improvement from 2018 to 2022. Maryland stands at 41st in terms of improvement compared to the rest of America, and it's clear why we have the narrative of the "Maryland Driver" in our heads.

As a Maryland driver, I offer now my perspective on a simple way the MVA programs can improve. First, when I studied under the Leap Driving Academy of Greenbelt, we didn't nearly have enough real life application to supplement all the quizzes we took. I suggest increasing the time spent on freeways and highways before class completion, so that newer drivers can get more experienced with what may become daily routes to their lives.

Car collision on I-695 in Baltimore County, resulting in the deaths of 6 highway workers. Image from NBC.


But to really get somewhere with this issue we have to fix our driving culture. In a WBAL article, Baltimore county police detective Tyler Rivers noted how Maryland drivers so comfortably exude an angry and disrespectful driving culture.

"On a daily basis, I've seen driving behavior of people switching lanes, unsafe speeding, not stopping at red lights," said Rivers. "Road rage incidents stem from aggressive driving behavior. I believe it has increased in the past two, three years."

Before we change fundamental aspects of the MVA program, I think it could benefit us a lot in an individual level to drive like we aren't the 8th worst drivers in America. It comes down to an individual choice to not put yourself and other people in danger, little things like not taking your phone out, not road raging, and genuinely acknowledging the responsibility you have to those around you when you're out on the road.
